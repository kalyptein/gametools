## Survival
---
### Finding Water

* In most of Dolmenwood, its many streams and springs make fresh water easy to come by.
	* A water bottle or skin can be kept full throughout the day.
* Some swamps and barren mirelands have unreliable access to potable water.
	* A waterskin (but not a smaller water bottle) carries enough water for a day in such places.  Roll WIS (Wildcraft) to find a water source, if any exists.
		* If spending the whole day looking for water (not traveling), take +A.

### Finding Food

* Choose whether to fish, forage, or hunt for the day, alongside normal travel.
	* If spending the whole day looking for food (not traveling), take +A.
* A single character rolls WIS (Wildcraft) DC 18.
	* If the party splits up, each group can roll, but also checks for encounters abd getting lost.
	* Modifiers can apply for season, location, etc.

Fishing
* Requires Fishing Gear (1 slot).
* Can be done in any hex with a lake or river.
* Success: 2d6 rations.  Roll for type of catch.
	* For each additional person with Fishing Gear who can participate, roll and extra d6, using the two highest d6 rolls for the result.

Foraging
* Success: 1d6 rations (1d4 in winter, 1d8 in autumn).  Roll for type of find (even odds of finding plants or fungi, roll type).
	* In hexes with special herbs or fungi, they may also be found.

Hunting
* Success: you have found wild game.  Roll for type and number of quarry.
	* Roll DEX (Stealth) DC (11+animal's WIS) to sneak up Nearby.  On failure, you are Far.
	* You must kill the animal, and have surprise on your attack.  Ordinary beasts are likely to flee when able.
	* Small animals yield 1 ration per HP, Medium animals yield 2 rations per HP, and Large animals yield 4 rations per HP.

### Making Fire

* Under normal circumstances, success is automatic.
* If factor(s) complicate fire-making, roll WIS (Wildcraft) DC 12 + 3 per additional factor.
	* Factors: precipitation, heavy precipitation, gathering wood in wetlands, high winds
	* Packing in your own firewood instead of gathering while making camp can remove some factors.
* If a roll is required, this takes up the party's camp actions.

### Shelter

* With extra time (a travel action), a party can find or make shelter to compensate for lack of tents and/or bedrolls.
	* Tent: WIS (Wildcraft) DC 15 (+3 in open terrain) to find a cave, thick tree canopy, or overhang, or make a crude shelter.
	* Bedroll: WIS (Wildcraft) DC 15 (+3 in rocky or marshy terrain): to find soft, dry ground or build up a foundation to sleep on.

### Tracking

* Search a Near area for tracks.
	* If found, you know the number and type of creature(s) (if at all familiar).  You recognize traits that affect gait, weight, and trail (limping, being heavily laden, bleeding, etc).
	* You can reconstruct a rough impression of events (fighting, made camp, went to a hidey-hole and came back, etc).
	* You can follow the tracks.  If circumstances worse (raising the DC), make a new check to continue, or else lose the trail.
* With time, training, and nominal conditions, success is automatic.
* If factor(s) complicate tracking, roll WIS (Wildcraft) DC 12 + 3 per additional factor.
	* Factors: untrained in tracking, in a rush, ground is hard, ground is rocky, small creature, each day since tracks were made, each hour of precipitation, dim light, quarry makes active efforts to cover trail its trail, quarry is trained in wildcraft (or is a naturally subtle creature).
	* Bonuses (-3 DC each): soft ground, ideal surface (fresh snow, smooth dust, etc), large group (10+), large creature, leaving a trail (bleeding, etc)
* You can't retry in the same location.  If you lose a trail, you may be able to intuit a spot further along and try again later.

## Skullduggery

### Lockpicking

* Given time (and adequate lighting), training, and tools, picking an average lock is automatic and 10 minutes.
	* For a rush job (1 round), roll with +D.
		* You may retry once (still with +D) with 1 minute of work.  If you fail, retrying takes the full, normal time.
	* With improvised tools or poor light, roll with +D.
		* On a failure, improvised tools are ruined and must be replaced.
	* Without training, you can only try once (until you improve, as for a Complex lock).
* Complex locks are high quality, enchanted, or otherwise involved.
	* With time, training, and tools, you can make a normal roll.
		* Without all three, the lock cannot be picked.
		* You can't retry until you gain a level, increase your DEX, or gain some other kind of advantage.
* Difficult locks (which are usually also Complex) require a roll, with +D, unless you can work on them during a Respite (roll normally).

stealthy scouting

	* mitigates failures (which typically result in a complete alert-raising clusterfuck; now you can just cut the mission short, suggest that some suspicion was raised, offer a cost or ask for a clever idea, and only clusterfuck in the worst possible outcomes)
* narrate it out, reveal the information, answer some questions
* then cut back to the group


* go forward exploring until stealth is required, then roll.
	* as long as you keep succeeding, you get to go a bit further, the GM quickly narrating and laying out what you see.
	* each "beat" of exploration lets you investigate a new area or spend time drilling down on some feature.  sometimes the GM may reveal info quickly / for free because it's fairly unopposed, sometimes you'll need to buy the time with another stealth roll.
	* when you fail a roll, you can decide to retreat back to the party, or you can Push.  If you Push, you get to reroll and proceed as long as you keep succeeding.  But the next failure (which might be the immediate reroll) results in some level of discovery.
		* Failure by a small margin: you leave some evidence or get glimpsed.  Alert / suspicion it raised, but you then get clear.  Bigger margin of failure means you're fully spotted / chased.
* you can walk into a true trap that you weren't aware of, but otherwise when you retreat, you get back out safely.

## Lore

### Musing

* Smoke a bowl of pipeweed over the course of a Brief or Short Rest while pondering a question, dilemma, or course of action.
	* Certain blends are better for certain matters, and perhaps worse for others.
* The GM may offer you a hint, answer, or +A on an INT check.

### Recalling Lore

* A given piece of lore has a rank:
	* Famous
		* Even those with no familiarity with the subject may know this.
			* e.g. Even those without any faerie lore may still know the fey don't like iron.
	* Common
		* Anyone with familiarity with the subject can automatically recall this.
			* e.g. Any peasant knows the local area.  Any wizard knows basic spell lore or the names of famous adepts.
		* Someone with a decent education or some tangential contact with the subject can make an INT roll to try to recall this.
			* e.g. A peddler who passes through the village might know the area.  An educated cleric or a wizard's bodyguard might know famous adepts.
	* Obscure
		* Information that requires familiarity to have a hope of recalling.
			* With enough time and/or a good source of information (an archive, library, network of informants, etc), it can automatically be recalled.
			* A suitably informed person may attempt an INT check to recall it promptly (Musing may help).
			* e.g. The weakness of a particular breed of demon.  Details of a minor noble lineage.
	* Secret
		* Information that must be learned through play by accessing a source that knows it (a teacher, tome, library, very knowledgeable practitioner, direct experimentation, etc).
